Marine Plastic Pollution

Simulation 1:Marine Plastic pollution-

Marine plastic pollution is a severe crisis faced globally in the current era. The crisis has been studied from the late 1960s-1970’s. Plastic is a synthetic material that was introduced during the industrial revolution, as a cheap, malleable and convenient source. Plastic is created utilising an artificial polymer (naturally occuring material found in hair and silk) made through the rearrangement of crude oil components, that provide plastic with its extremely useful properties. During the industrial revolution, this material was brought for mass production, as it did not require any manual labour and was available in large scales. Soon, scientists discovered that such plastic products require 500-1000 years to decompose, as synthetic polymer is an extremely durable material. As the consumption, utilisation and production of plastics became further developed in the sector of packaging, one with the requirement of high disposal, the situation escalated to radically impactful levels.

Some other major forms of plastic utilisation that result in marine plastic pollution include:

Improper disposal of packaging

Improper disposal of plastic cutlery

Improper disposal of plastic fibers such as single use bags

Improper disposal of plastic bottles and caps

Improper disposal of plastic straws

Improper disposal of food covers

Casting of ghost gear

Illegal dumping of various items

Inadequate waste management by governments

Littering in various locations

Contemporarily, when plastic is disposed of, it is thrown away in mass areas of garbage collection known as landfills. Such landfills emit environmentally hazardous chemicals and can reach groundwater through processes such as precipitation. The groundwater leads to streams, which subsequently leads to ponds, then to lakes, and rivers and ultimately to the ocean. At this point, the plastics would have broken down into various smaller pieces known as microplastics, through the effects of wave pressure and sunlight temperature, which pose higher threats to marine life. The microplastics are carried by slow-transitioning currents that carry approximately 1.9 million of such pieces per square meter. These same currents were originally proposed towards providing deep sea creatures with oxygen and nutrients. Microplastic contamination affects the flow of this process.

he natural circular motion of the ocean leads such ocean currents containing microplastics into vortexes known as gyres. Improper disposal near coastal regions have greater pronity towards this. There are 5 major rotating ocean currents (gyres). These are the North Atlantic gyre, South Atlantic gyre, Indian ocean gyre, North Pacific gyre and South Pacific gyre. Each of these systems is surrounded by various sub-gyres that also have many levels of plastic concentration. Including all of such gyres, today, there are approximately 5 trillion pieces of plastic floating in the ocean. Some major effects of marine plastic pollution involve:

Marine species suffocation through microplastic consumption

Marine species entanglement in ghost gear and other

Starvation among sea birds and fish, due to plastic’s nature of providing an illusion of being full

Repercussions on human health

Travelling of disease up the network of food chain

Expensive cleaning procedures

Accumulation of plastic on algae, causing production of foul odours that disturb flow of marine life

lteration of reproduction processes

Toxin infused pollutants affect organs
