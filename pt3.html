Climate Change

Climate change is a prevalent issue in the contemporary world. Global warming or climate change is a long term repercussion on the climatic conditions on the Earth. Greenhouse gases produced by human activities and utilisation of plastic products are a major contributor to global warming, due to effects on the density and function of the ozone layer. Studies have revealed that plastic production and product manufacturing industries have been among the leaders of greenhouse gas production.

The stages of this process are as follows:

Extraction of plastic through crude oil-

Primarily, crude oil is extracted from underground reservoirs utilising drilling and pumping methods. Crude oil is a material with great ease of extraction, as it consists of a comparatively lesser density and manageable temperature and pressure. Such extraction can be performed in water bodies as well. This is when water at high pressure is injected into the reservoirs and collected into wells. Crude oil extraction has a potential risk of contaminating streams and groundwater (this threatens access to clean drinking water, and the habitats of animals) while producing emissions that can further escalate the situation of climate change. Extraction poses an additional risk of oil spills, which have been known to be the most devastating hazard to marine life. Production of plastic-

The crude oil is transported to necessary facilities for plastic production. Firstly, the extracted raw material, crude oil is refined in a furnace. The refining process can enable the creation of monomers (a molecule in polymers), which are a crucial element in plastic. The crude oil is then transported to a distillation unit, wherein the crude oil is divided into fractions, one of which is naphtha, a building block of plastic. Next, the monomers created are chemically bonded into chains and converted into higher molecular weight hydrocarbons, essentially transforming them into polymers. Processes known as addition polymerisation and condensation polymerisation, both requiring the aid of catalysts. Then, the final stage of the process- compounding begins, wherein various materials are combined to create the desired result of plastic. The entire process of plastic production itself creates several emissions that harm environmental sustainability and ecological balance.

Plastic product manufacturing

The plastic is transported from facilities to the respective industries. The melted plastic is injected into the necessary steel molds for the product. The malleability of plastic and its average melting point enables this process to go smoothly. The injection moulding, if done with improper machinery may lead to emission production.

Product sale

The plastic products are sold in the market for various purposes such as packaging and every-day objects. Such products are considerably cheaper than products of other materials,

Consumer activity

The consumers utilise the products for their respective purposes.

Disposal and Waste industry

The products are often disposed of and carried to landfills, where they require 500-1000 years to decompose. The lightweight plastic fiber products are often blown away and are scattered into drainage systems and water bodies. Other plastic materials that end up in the landfills are susceptible to soil degradation and aquatic life contamination. Often, such plastic is burned in landfills for energy production to be sent to homes. This decreases the volume of waste but increases the intensity of environmental impact.

Climate change

Research reveals that for every ounce of PET, approximately 1 ounce of Co2 is produced. An imbalance of Co2 in the ecosystem can prevent the formation of new ozone layer molecules in the troposphere. The reduction of ozone layer density can change its complete functioning of UV radiation blocking. This leaves our planet vulnerable to excess temperature rise, altering sea levels and gradually leading to complete ecosystem imbalance and a gradual end to every unadaptable species on this planet that were once key to our ecosystem.
